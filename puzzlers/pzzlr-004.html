The Trouble With Traits

Contributed by Andrew Phillips & Nermin Serifovic
Source stackoverflow
Tested with Scala version 2.9.2

What does the following code print?

trait A {
  val foo: Int
  val bar = 10
  println("In A: foo: " + foo + ", bar: " + bar)
}

class B1 extends A {
  val foo = 25
  println("In B1: foo: " + foo + ", bar: " + bar)
}

class B2(val foo: Int = 25) extends A {
  println("In B2: foo: " + foo + ", bar: " + bar)
}

new B1
new B2

  1. In A: foo: 25, bar: 10
In B1: foo: 25, bar: 10
In A: foo: 25, bar: 10
In B2: foo: 25, bar: 10

  2. In A: foo: 0, bar: 10
In B1: foo: 25, bar: 10
In A: foo: 0, bar: 10
In B2: foo: 25, bar: 10

  3. In A: foo: 0, bar: 10
In B1: foo: 25, bar: 10
In A: foo: 25, bar: 10
In B2: foo: 25, bar: 10

  4. In A: foo: 25, bar: 10
In B1: foo: 25, bar: 10
In A: foo: 0, bar: 10
In B2: foo: 25, bar: 10

Explanation

When instantiating superclasses or traits, the parent constructor can be visualized as being executed before the first line of the child constructor but after the class definition.
